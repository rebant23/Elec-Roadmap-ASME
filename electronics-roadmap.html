⚡Analog Electronics

Beginner

What is it?

Analog electronics deals with signals that vary continuously — voltage and current that sweep smoothly rather than snapping between 0 and 1. Think of the sound wave from a guitar pickup, the gradual change in a temperature sensor's voltage, or the way a radio signal looks on an oscilloscope.

This is the bedrock of every electronic system. Even the most advanced digital芯片 still has analog front-ends, power regulators, and signal conditioning circuits. If you understand analog, you understand what's actually happening at the wire level.

Key concepts include resistive circuits, capacitors and inductors, diodes, transistors (BJT and MOSFET), operational amplifiers (op-amps), filters, and basic power supply design.

Why learn it?

  • Sensor interfacing — almost every sensor outputs an analog signal that needs conditioning before a microcontroller can read it.
  • Audio electronics — amplifiers, mixers, and equalizers are pure analog circuits.
  • Power supplies — every device needs regulated DC power; analog design makes that happen.
  • Communication systems — RF circuits, modulators, and receivers are analog at their core.
  • Medical devices — ECG, EEG, and pulse oximeters all start with analog signal conditioning.

Prerequisites

  • Basic understanding of voltage, current, resistance (Ohm's Law)
  • KCL and KVL
  • No programming needed yet

🔲Digital Electronics

Beginner

What is it?

Digital electronics works with discrete signals — things that are either HIGH or LOW, 1 or 0. It's the logic layer that sits on top of analog circuits and makes computation possible. Every smartphone, computer, and microcontroller in existence is built on digital logic.

You'll encounter logic gates (AND, OR, NOT, XOR), flip-flops, registers, multiplexers, decoders, and counters. These are the building blocks of everything from a simple LED blinker to a modern processor. Understanding how they work gives you an intuition for what's happening inside every chip you'll ever use.

Why learn it?

  • Foundation of computing — CPUs, GPUs, and microcontrollers are all digital circuits at their core.
  • FPGA and hardware design — you literally build digital circuits when you program an FPGA.
  • Understanding datasheets — digital logic levels, timing diagrams, and truth tables appear in every component's documentation.
  • Protocol design — communication protocols like SPI and I²C are defined by digital timing.

Prerequisites

  • Basic analog electronics (voltage levels, pull-up/pull-down resistors)

⌨️Programming in C

Beginner

What is it?

C is the language that runs the world's hardware. Every microcontroller firmware, every operating system kernel, every embedded device — C is there. It's close to the metal: you manage memory directly, you work with pointers, you talk to hardware registers.

Unlike Python or JavaScript, C doesn't hide the computer from you. That's not a bug — it's the entire point. Learning C teaches you how computers actually work, and that knowledge transfers to every other language you'll ever learn.

Why learn it?

  • Every microcontroller runs C — STM32, ESP32, Arduino (under the hood), PIC, AVR — all C.
  • Memory management — understanding pointers and memory allocation is a superpower in embedded.
  • Performance — when you need deterministic, fast code, C is the answer.
  • Industry standard — embedded systems, automotive, aerospace, medical devices all use C.
  • Reads datasheets — register maps and bit manipulation make sense when you know C.

Prerequisites

  • No prior programming required (but it helps if you've seen any language before)

🔧Embedded Systems

Beginner

What is it?

An embedded system is a computer designed to do one specific job, built into a larger device. Your washing machine, your car's engine controller, the thermostat on your wall, the flight controller in a drone — all embedded systems. They run firmware (software burned into hardware) and typically have resource constraints that desktop software never faces.

This is where electronics meets programming. You'll work with microcontrollers — small, affordable chips that have a CPU, memory, and peripherals (GPIO, ADC, timers, UART) all on a single package. The Arduino platform is the most common entry point, but the real industry runs on bare-metal and HAL-level development.

Why learn it?

  • Everything is embedded — there are more microcontrollers manufactured each year than people on Earth.
  • Career opportunities — embedded engineers are in high demand in automotive, aerospace, medical, and consumer electronics.
  • Cross-disciplinary — you work with hardware, firmware, and sometimes networking simultaneously.
  • Physical results — you see your code control real-world things: lights, motors, displays, sensors.

Prerequisites

  • Basic C programming
  • Basic digital electronics
💡 Arduino vs. Bare Metal

Arduino is a great way to start — it abstracts away complexity so you can focus on logic. But once you're comfortable, try writing bare-metal C for an STM32 or AVR. That's where the real learning happens.

🎯STM32

Intermediate

What is it?

STM32 is a family of ARM Cortex-M microcontrollers made by STMicroelectronics. They're the industry workhorse — found in everything from industrial controllers to consumer devices. Compared to Arduino's 8-bit AVR, STM32 gives you 32-bit processing power, more peripherals, higher clock speeds, and a professional development environment (STM32CubeIDE).

Learning STM32 means working with hardware registers, configuring peripherals through registers or HAL (Hardware Abstraction Layer), handling interrupts, timers, PWM, ADC, and DMA. This is real embedded development — the kind you'd do at a job.

Why learn it?

  • Industry standard — STM32 dominates the microcontroller market in professional embedded.
  • Powerful peripherals — advanced timers, multiple ADCs, DAC, USB, Ethernet, CAN, SPI, I²C.
  • Scalable — from simple F0 series to powerful H7 series with dual cores and FPU.
  • Ecosystem — STM32CubeMX for pin configuration, HAL/LL libraries, extensive documentation.
  • Job-ready skill — listing "STM32 development" on your resume gets interviews.

Prerequisites

  • Strong C programming (pointers, structs, bit manipulation)
  • Basic embedded systems concepts (GPIO, interrupts, timers)
  • Familiarity with datasheets and reference manuals

📡ESP32 / IoT

Intermediate

What is it?

The ESP32 is a Wi-Fi and Bluetooth-enabled microcontroller made by Espressif. It's cheap (~$3), powerful (dual-core, 240 MHz), and packed with features. It's the go-to chip for Internet of Things projects — connecting physical devices to the internet.

IoT (Internet of Things) is the practice of embedding sensors, actuators, and networking into everyday objects. Smart home devices, agricultural monitoring, industrial sensors, wearables — IoT is everywhere. The ESP32 makes it accessible because it handles networking out of the box.

Why learn it?

  • Connected devices — send sensor data to the cloud, receive commands remotely, monitor things from your phone.
  • Smart home — build your own smart switches, environmental monitors, and home automation systems.
  • Low cost — ESP32 dev boards cost $3-10, making experimentation risk-free.
  • Wi-Fi + Bluetooth — dual wireless interfaces in a single chip.
  • Huge community — extensive libraries, tutorials, and community support.

Prerequisites

  • Basic embedded systems (or Arduino experience)
  • Basic C or MicroPython
  • Understanding of HTTP and basic networking helps

🔗Communication Protocols

Intermediate

What is it?

Communication protocols are the rules that govern how electronic devices talk to each other. When a sensor sends data to a microcontroller, when a microcontroller sends commands to a motor driver, when two boards exchange information — they're all using protocols.

The three most common you'll use daily are UART (serial, simple, point-to-point), SPI (fast, full-duplex, uses multiple chip select lines), and I²C (two-wire, supports multiple devices on one bus). More advanced protocols like CAN (automotive), USB, and Ethernet appear in specific applications.

Why learn it?

  • Everything communicates — sensors, displays, memory, other MCUs — they all need a protocol.
  • Datasheet literacy — every datasheet tells you which protocol a device uses. Knowing them means you can read any datasheet.
  • Debugging — logic analyzers and oscilloscopes show you protocol signals. Understanding the protocol means you can debug hardware issues.
  • System design — choosing the right protocol for a job is a fundamental design skill.

Prerequisites

  • Basic embedded systems / microcontroller experience
  • C programming

👁️Sensors

Intermediate

What is it?

Sensors are the eyes, ears, and skin of any electronic system. They convert physical phenomena — light, temperature, pressure, motion, distance, sound — into electrical signals that a microcontroller can process. Without sensors, a robot is blind, a weather station is useless, and a self-driving car is just a car.

You'll encounter a wide range: IMUs (accelerometers + gyroscopes) for motion tracking, encoders for motor position, ultrasonic and LIDAR sensors for distance, cameras for vision, hall effect sensors for magnetic fields, and countless others.

Why learn it?

  • Robotics perception — robots need sensors to navigate, avoid obstacles, and interact with the world.
  • Data acquisition — any measurement system starts with the right sensor.
  • IoT monitoring — environmental sensors are the backbone of smart agriculture, smart cities, and industrial monitoring.
  • Fusion — combining multiple sensors (sensor fusion) is a core skill for autonomous systems.

Prerequisites

  • Embedded systems fundamentals
  • Communication protocols (I²C, SPI, UART)
  • Basic analog electronics (for analog sensors)

🔋Power Electronics

Intermediate

What is it?

Power electronics is about managing electrical energy efficiently — converting voltage levels, regulating power, and driving high-current loads. Every device that plugs into a wall or runs on a battery uses power electronics: voltage regulators, DC-DC converters (buck, boost, buck-boost), battery management systems, and motor drivers.

The key challenge is efficiency. Linear regulators waste energy as heat. Switching regulators use inductors and capacitors to convert power at 85-95% efficiency. Understanding this distinction — and when to use each — is fundamental.

Why learn it?

  • Every project needs power — choosing the wrong regulator means overheating, battery drain, or damaged components.
  • Battery-powered devices — efficient power conversion extends battery life dramatically.
  • Motor driving — motors need high current and often different voltages than your logic.
  • EVs and renewable energy — power electronics is the backbone of electric vehicles and solar systems.

Prerequisites

  • Analog electronics (transistors, inductors, capacitors)
  • Basic understanding of switching circuits

⚙️Motor Control

Intermediate

What is it?

Motors convert electrical energy into mechanical motion. There are several types, each with different characteristics: DC motors (simple, fast, need drivers), stepper motors (precise positioning, open-loop), servo motors (angle control via PWM), and BLDC motors (high efficiency, used in drones and EVs).

Motor control goes beyond just spinning a motor. It involves driving circuits (H-bridges, MOSFETs), PWM speed control, closed-loop feedback with encoders, and advanced techniques like Field-Oriented Control (FOC) for BLDC motors.

Why learn it?

  • Robotics — every moving part in a robot has a motor behind it.
  • Industrial automation — conveyor belts, CNC machines, robotic arms.
  • Electric vehicles — BLDC and FOC motor control is the core technology in EVs.
  • Drones — quadcopters use four BLDC motors with precise speed control.

Prerequisites

  • Power electronics basics (MOSFETs, H-bridges)
  • Embedded systems (PWM generation, interrupts)
  • C programming

📐PCB Design

Intermediate

What is it?

PCB (Printed Circuit Board) design is the process of turning a schematic diagram into a physical board that connects components with copper traces. It's how every electronic product goes from concept to manufacturing. You design the board layout, define copper paths, place components, and generate manufacturing files (Gerbers) that a fab house uses to produce your board.

KiCad is the go-to free, open-source EDA (Electronic Design Automation) tool. It's professional-grade, used by companies and hobbyists alike. Learning KiCad means you can design boards from simple breakout boards to complex multi-layer designs.

Why learn it?

  • From breadboard to product — PCBs make your circuits permanent, compact, and professional.
  • Job skill — hardware engineers design PCBs daily. It's a core competency.
  • Cost-effective — PCB fabrication is cheap ($2 for 5 boards from JLCPCB). Prototyping is accessible.
  • Portfolio piece — showing boards you designed is powerful in interviews.
  • Integration — combine sensors, microcontrollers, and power circuits into one custom board.

Prerequisites

  • Analog and digital electronics basics
  • Ability to read schematics
  • Understanding of the components you want to place

🎛️Control Systems

Intermediate

What is it?

Control systems engineering is about making things behave the way you want. A cruise control system maintains speed. A drone stays level in wind. A robotic arm reaches a precise position. All of these use feedback control — measuring the current state, comparing it to the desired state, and adjusting accordingly.

The most important concept you'll learn is the PID controller (Proportional-Integral-Derivative). It's the most widely used control algorithm in the world. You'll also encounter transfer functions, stability analysis, Bode plots, and state-space representations.

Why learn it?

  • Robotics — every robot joint, every drone motor, every self-balancing system needs control.
  • Industrial automation — manufacturing plants run on PID controllers everywhere.
  • Automotive — ABS, traction control, cruise control, lane keeping — all control systems.
  • It's elegant — understanding why a system oscillates, then fixing it with math, is deeply satisfying.

Prerequisites

  • Basic math (calculus helps, but PID can be understood intuitively)
  • Basic programming (Python or C)
  • Some experience with sensors and actuators

🤖Robotics

Intermediate

What is it?

Robotics is the integration of mechanical engineering, electronics, and software to create machines that can sense their environment, make decisions, and take action. It's the ultimate multidisciplinary field — it combines sensors, motors, control systems, programming, and mechanical design into one coherent system.

At its core, robotics is about the loop: sense → think → act. Sensors gather data. A controller processes that data and makes decisions. Actuators (motors, servos) execute the decisions. Understanding and optimizing this loop is the essence of robotics engineering.

Why learn it?

  • The ultimate integration — robotics forces you to apply everything you've learned in one project.
  • Industry growth — robotics is one of the fastest-growing engineering fields globally.
  • Diverse applications — from manufacturing and healthcare to space exploration and agriculture.
  • Fun factor — nothing beats watching a robot you built navigate a room on its own.

Prerequisites

  • Embedded systems (microcontrollers, sensors, motors)
  • Basic control systems (PID at minimum)
  • C or Python programming

🏗️ROS 2

Advanced

What is it?

ROS 2 (Robot Operating System 2) is middleware — a framework that provides the tools, libraries, and conventions for building robot software. It handles communication between different parts of a robot (nodes), sensor data management, hardware abstraction, and provides a rich ecosystem of pre-built packages for navigation, SLAM, perception, and more.

ROS 2 is the industry standard for robot software development. It runs on Linux (typically Ubuntu) and uses DDS (Data Distribution Service) for communication. It's not an operating system in the traditional sense — it's a development framework that runs on top of Linux.

Why learn it?

  • Industry standard — most professional robot companies use ROS/ROS 2.
  • Modular design — build separate nodes for perception, planning, and control, then connect them.
  • Simulation — Gazebo integration lets you test robots virtually before building hardware.
  • Navigation stack — pre-built SLAM and path planning algorithms you can use immediately.
  • Community — thousands of open-source packages you can use and learn from.

Prerequisites

  • Linux command line (Ubuntu basics)
  • Python or C++ programming
  • Basic understanding of robotics concepts (sensors, actuators, coordinate frames)

📷Computer Vision

Advanced

What is it?

Computer vision is the field of making computers understand images and video. It encompasses everything from simple color detection to deep-learning-based object recognition. CV is how self-driving cars see pedestrians, how phones recognize faces, and how drones navigate autonomously.

OpenCV is the foundational library — it provides thousands of algorithms for image processing, feature detection, object tracking, and more. Modern CV also uses deep learning (neural networks) for tasks like image classification, object detection, and semantic segmentation.

Why learn it?

  • Autonomous systems — self-driving cars, drones, and robots all rely on vision.
  • Industrial inspection — automated quality control, defect detection, sorting systems.
  • Medical imaging — X-ray analysis, surgical assistance, diagnostic tools.
  • AR/VR — augmented reality applications need real-time vision processing.
  • Security — surveillance, face recognition, anomaly detection.

Prerequisites

  • Python programming
  • Basic linear algebra (helpful, not strictly required for OpenCV)
  • Linux basics (recommended for GPU-accelerated work)

🐧Embedded Linux

Advanced

What is it?

Embedded Linux means running the Linux operating system on resource-constrained hardware — single-board computers like the Raspberry Pi, BeagleBone, or industrial boards running ARM processors. Unlike microcontrollers where you write bare-metal firmware, embedded Linux gives you a full operating system with process management, networking, file systems, and hardware drivers.

You'll work with board bring-up, custom Linux images (using Buildroot or Yocto), device drivers, cross-compilation, and system integration. It's where embedded systems meets software engineering.

Why learn it?

  • Complex systems — when you need networking, databases, USB, cameras, and GUIs, a microcontroller isn't enough.
  • Industrial applications — kiosks, medical devices, automotive infotainment, industrial controllers all run Linux.
  • Robotics — many robot controllers run ROS 2 on embedded Linux (Jetson, BeagleBone, Raspberry Pi).
  • Security and updates — Linux provides networking stacks, security features, and OTA update capabilities.

Prerequisites

  • Linux command line proficiency
  • C programming
  • Basic embedded systems understanding

⏱️RTOS

Advanced

What is it?

A Real-Time Operating System (RTOS) is a lightweight OS designed for embedded systems that need deterministic timing — tasks must complete within strict deadlines. Unlike a general-purpose OS (like Linux), an RTOS guarantees that high-priority tasks will execute within a known, bounded time.

FreeRTOS is the most widely used RTOS in the world — it's built into ESP-IDF, STM32CubeMX, and supported by every major microcontroller vendor. You'll learn about tasks, semaphores, mutexes, message queues, task priorities, and scheduling.

Why learn it?

  • Safety-critical systems — medical devices, automotive, aerospace all require real-time guarantees.
  • Multi-tasking — read sensors while controlling motors while communicating — all simultaneously.
  • Industry standard — FreeRTOS is used in millions of commercial products.
  • Clean architecture — RTOS teaches you to design modular, interruptible, prioritized systems.

Prerequisites

  • Embedded systems (STM32 or ESP32 experience)
  • C programming (especially concurrency concepts)
  • Understanding of interrupts and polling

Create a system where three tasks run concurrently: sensor reading (high priority), display update (medium priority), and data logging (low priority). Use semaphores to share data between tasks. Teaches RTOS task management and synchronization.

🧩FPGA

Advanced

What is it?

An FPGA (Field-Programmable Gate Array) is an integrated circuit that you can configure after manufacturing. Unlike a microcontroller that executes instructions sequentially, an FPGA lets you build custom digital circuits in hardware — you're literally wiring up logic gates, flip-flops, and routing in parallel.

You describe hardware using HDL (Hardware Description Language) — typically Verilog or VHDL. The FPGA toolchain synthesizes your description into actual logic circuits on the chip. This is fundamentally different from software programming — you're designing hardware, not writing instructions.

Why learn it?

  • Massive parallelism — process thousands of data streams simultaneously, something CPUs can't match.
  • Low latency — deterministic, nanosecond-level response times for critical applications.
  • Custom hardware accelerators — accelerate specific algorithms 10-100x compared to software.
  • High-frequency trading, radar, medical imaging — industries where speed matters most.
  • Computer architecture — building a CPU on an FPGA teaches you computer architecture better than any textbook.

Prerequisites

  • Digital electronics (logic gates, flip-flops, state machines)
  • Some programming experience
  • Willingness to think in hardware, not software
⚠️ Mindset Shift

FPGA development is hardware design, not software. Everything runs in parallel by default. You need to think in terms of circuits and timing, not functions and loops. This is the hardest mental shift in this entire roadmap — but also the most rewarding.

🧠AI for Robotics

Advanced

What is it?

AI for robotics combines machine learning with robotic systems to enable perception, decision-making, and autonomous behavior that goes beyond hand-crafted rules. This includes reinforcement learning (robots learning by trial and error), deep learning for perception (understanding images and point clouds), and planning algorithms that let robots reason about their actions.

This field is rapidly evolving. Modern robots use neural networks for object detection, natural language processing for human-robot interaction, and learned policies for manipulation tasks. It's where cutting-edge AI research meets real-world physical systems.

Why learn it?

  • Autonomous vehicles — self-driving cars use deep learning for perception and decision-making.
  • Manipulation — robots that can pick up arbitrary objects need AI-powered grasp planning.
  • Search and rescue — robots that navigate disaster zones need adaptive, intelligent behavior.
  • Future-proofing — AI is transforming every field of robotics, and the demand for talent is exploding.

Prerequisites

  • Python programming
  • ROS 2 basics
  • Computer vision fundamentals
  • Linear algebra and basic statistics (for ML)
